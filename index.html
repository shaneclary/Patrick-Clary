The Work

From combat medic to hospice physician.

  1. 1969–1970

    Vietnam

    A Quaker and conscientious objector, he chose to serve without a weapon — as a combat medic with U.S. infantry units. He met death early, and learned to stay.

  2. Georgetown

    Poetry before medicine

    His first professional training was as a poet, in the Georgetown workshops of Roland Flint. Then came Georgetown’s School of Medicine. He has never stopped doing both.

  3. Family practice

    New Hampshire

    Board certified in family practice: decades of the ordinary, essential work of caring for people and the families around them.

  4. 1993

    Hospice

    He turned toward the work that defines him — medicine for the dying, and for the people who love them.

  5. Leadership

    A voice for the field

    He has served as president of the New Hampshire Hospice and Palliative Care Organization, helping carry palliative care forward across the state.

  6. 2003

    Death Valley

    He led a group of hospice workers into the desert for a Vision Fast, in the tradition of Steven Foster and Meredith Little of the School of Lost Borders. Out of that silence came a book.

  7. 2006

    Dying for Beginners

    Lost Borders Press published his lyrical memoir in poems — love, war, music, loss — from the Nevada desert to Southeast Asia, from Jonestown to the big-city ER.

  8. Dover, N.H.

    One bedside at a time

    Board certified in hospice and palliative medicine. More than four decades of practice — still measured the same way it began.

Philosophy

He keeps the instructions short.

Say goodbye.

Forgive.

Ask forgiveness.

Give thanks.

Say: I love you.

The five tasks of the dying — and of the living — at the heart of his poem “Five Tasks Taught by Hospice Nurses.”

Presence

He understands that showing up — and staying — is itself a treatment.

Language

A poem in a medical journal is a prescription of a different kind. He writes them anyway, and the journals keep printing them.

Healing

Healing is not always curing. Some of the most important medicine begins after cure is off the table.

Nature

The desert and the woods.

Some medicine happens outdoors. In 2003 he took hospice workers into Death Valley to fast, watch, and listen — and Dying for Beginners began there. What the desert teaches and what the bedside teaches turn out to be the same lesson, at different volumes: pay attention; you are not in charge; everything that matters is already here.

Between the Nevada desert and the New Hampshire woods runs the whole geography of his practice — walking, silence, and the patience of trees.

Stories

The heart of this archive.

The truest record of Patrick’s work is the people changed by unhurried hours at a bedside — patients, families, nurses, students, colleagues, friends. If he has sat with you, taught you, or walked with you: write it down. It belongs here.
